# Ideas Session — 2026-07-13 18:15

## Phase 0 — Context

Martin Mikkelsen asked to survey recent explicitly correlated Gaussian (ECG)
work in few-body physics and use it to identify ways FewBodyECG.jl could tackle
harder problems. His established background is computational physics, ECGs,
Julia, and tensor-train/quantics tensor-train methods. The current repository
now supports target-state optimisation and a hydrogen 1s/2s/2p/3d comparison.

An older ideas session focused on generalized pairwise interactions; those are
now largely represented in the package. No advisor registry, indexed project
literature, or specialist paper-search connector is configured, so this
session will use current primary-source web searches.

## Phase 1 — Survey scope

The survey will cover 2021–2026 developments in correlated-Gaussian/ECG
methods across atomic, molecular, nuclear, and trapped/cold-atom few-body
physics. It will also search adjacent low-rank/tensor methods for structural
ideas that could make ECG calculations more scalable or more automated.

The working question is: which developments are both scientifically interesting
and realistic extensions of a Julia ECG codebase with target-state solvers,
analytic pairwise matrix elements, and emerging ForwardDiff optimisation?

## Phase 1 — Evidence checkpoint

The literature separates into four active streams. First, Fedorov, Teilmann,
Østerlund, and Norrbohm (2024, Few-Body Systems 65, 75,
doi:10.1007/s00601-024-01945-x) derive analytic overlap, kinetic, and Coulomb
matrix elements for rank-1 and rank-2 tensor-prefactor ECGs, including hydrogen
p and d tests. This directly validates the repository's new prefactors, but
also exposed a correctness/design issue in the current visualisation: the d
test is an xy component, so a z-axis cut is physically zero. Rank-1 and rank-2
states need a full N×3 coordinate evaluator and angular cuts or an angularly
averaged radial density; a scalar radial profile is only physically safe for
s-waves. Shomenov and Bubin (2023, Phys. Rev. E 108, 065308,
doi:10.1103/PhysRevE.108.065308) supply the complementary global-vector/
bipolar-harmonic route and analytic nonlinear-parameter derivatives.

Second, nuclear work is turning correlated Gaussians into an EFT and reaction
toolchain. Sun, Detmold, Luo, and Shanahan (2022, Phys. Rev. D 105, 074508,
doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.105.074508) combine finite-volume pionless EFT with
differentiable programming, reporting comparable accuracy with substantially
fewer basis functions than stochastic selection. Yaron et al. (2022, Phys.
Rev. D 106, 014511, doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.106.014511) give periodic CG-SVM for
light nuclei; Schäfer and Bazak (2023, Phys. Rev. C 107, 064001,
doi:10.1103/PhysRevC.107.064001) extract scattering information from trapped
few-nucleon systems; and Weiss-Attia, Schäfer, and Bazak (2024, Phys. Rev. D
109, 114515, doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.109.114515) offer a periodic pionless-EFT
blueprint. This suggests a composable `PeriodicBox`/trap/operator/observable
layer rather than another isolated energy solver.

Third, beyond-Coulomb and anisotropic applications are growing: deformed ECGs
are used for cavity-QED Hamiltonians (Beutel et al., 2021, JCP 155, 214103,
doi:10.1063/5.0066427); complex ECG packets and Rothe's method have recently
been used for multidimensional dynamics (Schrader, Pedersen, Kvaal, 2024,
arXiv:2411.05459); and cold-atom calculations continue to use Gaussian
finite-range interactions and traps. These are viable application directions,
but are larger engineering projects.

Fourth, the closest tensor-method literature is complementary rather than a
drop-in ECG replacement. Brice et al. (2025, Phys. Rev. A 111, 022812,
doi:10.1103/PhysRevA.111.022812) solve three-body Coulomb systems with
canonical/TT tensor representations in perimetric Laguerre coordinates, and
Brice et al. (2025, Mol. Phys., doi:10.1080/00268976.2025.2561867) extend this
to excited S states. This makes He/H-/Ps- a clean ECG-vs-TT benchmark suite.
Ritter et al. (2024, Phys. Rev. Lett. 132, 056501,
doi:10.1103/PhysRevLett.132.056501) develop quantics tensor cross
interpolation, suggesting an incremental bridge: compile a general radial
potential into a small validated Gaussian sum and retain analytic ECG matrix
elements. The claim that this is a novel ECG–QTT bridge remains an inference,
not an established literature result.

The synthesis should distinguish a solid package foundation—correct
symmetry-resolved observables, general tensor/global-vector states, and
hybrid SVM plus ForwardDiff optimisation—from bolder research directions:
Gaussian-sum potential compilation, periodic/trapped pionless EFT,
multi-state target-subspace optimisation, and ECG-vs-TT cross-validation.

## Phase 2 — Book-reproduction candidate

Martin chose to begin by reproducing results from Suzuki and Varga,
*Stochastic Variational Approach to Quantum-Mechanical Few-Body Problems*
(1998). Inspection of Sect. 4.2 identifies the positronium negative ion
Ps- as the first compact benchmark. The book treats e+e-e- with an
electron-singlet spatially symmetric correlated-Gaussian basis and quotes
the reference energy -0.262005 Ha. Table 4.5 compares K = 100 runs:
SVM with 10 candidates gives approximately -0.26199427 Ha, and one
10-candidate refinement sweep gives -0.26200231 Ha.

The existing public API represents the three particles directly as
`Operators([1, 1, 1], [+1, -1, -1])` with kinetic and Coulomb terms, and
already implements the book's competitive SVM and cyclic refinement pattern.
A diagnostic K = 100, n = 10 run revealed a sampling-scale mismatch rather
than a confirmed solver defect: the package's default scale 1 samples pair
lengths only in [0.02, 5], while the book's physical random ranges extend to
roughly 10--20 bohr. Holding every other parameter fixed, the energy changed
from -0.21274030 Ha at scale 1 to -0.26193606 Ha at scale 5; one current
refinement sweep reached -0.26196407 Ha. We should therefore first decide
whether to reproduce the historical table under its original random sampling
law, or establish a modern, deterministic Halton/Sobol reproduction with its
own documented acceptance criterion. No implementation change has been made.

## Phase 2 — Scope split

Martin approved the historically faithful Ps- route and asked for a
solid-state physics example afterwards. These are deliberately two separate
deliverables. The first must preserve the book's three independently sampled
pair-width ranges and a seeded random path, rather than silently tuning the
current shared `scale`; it validates the SVM/refinement machinery. A second
example should reuse the validated three-body Coulomb core if possible. The
leading candidate is a bulk effective-mass negative trion (two electrons and
one hole), which is the semiconductor analogue of Ps- with unequal masses
and dielectric-scaled Coulomb coupling. A genuinely periodic-solid example
would instead require periodic images, neutralisation, and a new finite-volume
operator layer, and should be scoped as a later independent project.

## Phase 2 — Correction: book solid-state benchmark

Martin clarified that the next example must come from the book's own
solid-state chapter, Chapter 10, not an independently chosen semiconductor
model. The chapter contains: 3D and 2D excitonic complexes (Sect. 10.1,
Tables 10.1--10.3), 2D harmonically confined quantum dots (Sect. 10.2,
Table 10.5), magnetic-field dots (Sect. 10.3), and 3D shifted-Gaussian
quantum dots (Sect. 10.4, Table 10.6). The repository's existing `ps2.jl`
already corresponds exactly to the 3D equal-mass biexciton/positronium
molecule entry in Table 10.1, even though it is named dipositronium. Its
-0.516003778 Ha reference is more precise than the table's -0.516.

The most meaningful new reproduction is therefore Table 10.5's two-electron
2D harmonic quantum dot at hbar*omega = 1, whose exact E = 3.000 target is
given in the text. Unlike the existing 3D Hooke's-atom example, it requires
generalising the ECG integrals and observables from fixed 3D to 2D. The
alternative Table 10.6 asks for shifted, antisymmetrised nonzero-L
three-to-five-electron states and is a substantially larger project.
